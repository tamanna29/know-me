The dilemma

I owned the Rightsizing engine, the part of CloudHealth that tells companies where they're overpaying for cloud they aren't using. It's a satisfying product to own on paper: the math is real, the savings are real, and the pitch is almost insultingly simple, here's money you're wasting, here's how to stop. So what bothered me was that the savings were sitting right there, fully calculated, and a lot of customers just weren't taking them.

The stakeholder explanation was tidy and confident: we were missing features. There was a list, more granular controls, more configurability, more depth, and the theory was that the next feature down the list would finally unlock adoption. It's a reasonable story. It's also the story you tell when you haven't looked closely, because “build more” is an easier answer than “something's wrong with what we already shipped.” The dilemma was whether to trust the list, or trust what the usage data was quietly telling me.

The KPI

The number I owned was feature adoption, were people actually using and acting on the recommendations? But adoption was a proxy. The outcome that actually mattered was realized savings per account. That distinction was the whole game: we could generate flawless recommendations forever, but if nobody acted, both numbers stayed flat. The product wasn't measured by whether it was right. It was measured by whether anyone did anything about it.

The thought process — and how I found it

Rather than open the feature backlog, I started with discovery. I pulled the product analytics to see where people actually dropped off, they'd view a recommendation and not act, so the funnel died at the moment of trust, not the moment of discovery. Then I went qualitative: I sat in on customer calls and partnered closely with our TAMs and support teams, who were sitting on the real signal, to hear how customers talked about the recommendations. I also worked on the testing and monitoring services behind the engine, so I could see how the recommendations behaved in production, not just how they looked. The language from customers wasn't “I need more options.” It was hesitation, “how did it get this number,” “is this safe to actually run.”

So I put myself in their seat. Would you reconfigure live production infrastructure because a dashboard told you to, with no idea how it decided, when you're the one who gets paged if it breaks? Of course not. The non-action wasn't a missing feature. It was a rational response to being asked for blind faith. We'd optimized hard for being right and forgotten to be believable, and those are different products. A correct recommendation nobody trusts delivers exactly zero dollars. So the work wasn't “add controls.” It was “make the reasoning visible.”

The PM craft — aligning and shipping

Having a contrarian insight is the easy part; getting an org to act on it is the job. I had to reprioritize against features stakeholders were actively asking for, so I led with evidence, not opinion. I brought the funnel data and the customer-call themes into a clear narrative, the savings exist, customers don't trust them, here's the proof, and used it to reframe the roadmap conversation from “which feature next” to “what's actually blocking adoption.” That turned a potential turf fight into alignment, because I wasn't asking them to take my word for it, I was showing them the drop-off.

From there I wrote the PRD around legibility, surfacing how each recommendation was generated, what it was based on, and why it was safe, and worked closely with engineering to scope it against real architectural constraints rather than an idealized version. I sequenced it deliberately: ship the explanation layer, instrument it, and prove the hypothesis before expanding scope, so we were never betting the roadmap on an untested belief. I ran it through standard sprint planning and kept customer success looped in so the people fielding the questions knew what was coming and could feed reactions back.

The harder part was the communication. Telling stakeholders their requested features were moving down the list could have cost trust, so I didn't deliver it as a no, I framed it as a sequencing decision tied to the goal they already cared about, and made the reasoning visible the same way the product made its recommendations visible. I tailored the message to the audience: a crisp data narrative for leadership, architectural tradeoffs for engineering, and plain talking points for the TAMs and support teams so they could explain the change to customers consistently. Throughout, I kept a tight feedback loop, regular check-ins, shared progress, and an open channel back from the customer-facing teams, so nobody was surprised and disagreements surfaced early while they were still cheap to resolve.

What changed, and the tradeoff

The uncomfortable call was deprioritizing features people explicitly wanted to fix a problem nobody had named. Telling a stakeholder “I'm not building your top items yet, I'm building trust instead” is not a fun conversation. But you can't feature your way out of a trust problem, and I was confident enough in the data to spend the political capital.

What made it defensible was that I validated it instead of asserting it, hypothesis-driven A/B testing and causal analysis in Pendo, so the outcome rested on evidence, not my conviction. Adoption rose 30%, we scaled cost governance across 50+ enterprise accounts, and it drove $24k+ in annualized savings per account, savings that had been available the entire time and were simply never believed.

The thread through everything I build: getting the answer right is the easy half. Getting a human to trust it enough to act is the actual job.
